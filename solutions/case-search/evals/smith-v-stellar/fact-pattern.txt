My client, Smith ("Smith"), lives in Texas and works in Texas. He signed a 2-year non-compete agreement that also details his compensation and employment info (the "Agreement"), with his company Stellar Company LLC ("Stellar").

Smith is a salesman and led the Stellar enterprise in sales. Most of Smith's compensation in the Agreement is from commissions, and his performance has been objectively excellent. But, the owner of Stellar, Joe Brown ("Joe"), didn't want to keep paying Smith such a high commission. Thus, Joe told Smith that Smith must take a new role as Sales Director that is salaried only. Joe sent Smith a new contract for that new job. Agreeing to the new contract terms would result in Smith being paid only half as much.

In the Agreement, there is a 2-year non-compete that bans work in Texas and 6 other states. This covers work with companies that are termed as 'competitors,' but the definition actually prohibits working in same industry, even for non-competitors (see below).

Smith refused to sign the contract for the new job. Stellar fired him, citing insubordination for not taking the new job and just because Joe no longer likes Smith. Smith founded a new business in the same industry (also in Texas), but one that sells a non-competitive product. Stellar is suing Smith for breach of contract, hoping to enforce the non-compete and issue an injunction plus damages. Smith is a 36-year old white male Christian, born/raised in U.S. as a U.S. citizen, and fully physically fit.
